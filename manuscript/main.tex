\begin{abstract}
Fine particulate matter (PM\textsubscript{2.5}) is a leading environmental risk factor for morbidity and mortality in urban areas. This study addresses \emph{when} parsimonious time-series models (AR / SARIMA) versus machine learning (ensembles, DeepAR) are appropriate for \emph{short-term} (one- to 24-hour) hourly PM\textsubscript{2.5} forecasting in Yerevan, Armenia, and how conclusions depend on spatial aggregation (city mean vs administrative districts vs station-level samples) and on temporal stability. After aligning data to a regular grid and controlled imputation (with optional sensitivity to medium-gap policy), we use leakage-safe features including lags, rolling moments, and calendar encodings, with strict chronological train/validation/test splits. \emph{Walk-forward} evaluation (primary interpretive frame) reports mean error and across-fold variance for a common model zoo, including SARIMA in each fold. Point metrics on a single test window are complemented by circular \emph{block} bootstrap CIs for MAE and Diebold--Mariano tests with a data-driven HAC lag. We also include a DeepAR benchmark; when valid prediction-interval columns are emitted, 90\% coverage and a Winkler score are reported in \texttt{results\_summary.json}. A recent AUA capstone monograph on air quality in Yerevan \citep{auaair2025yerevan} offers useful local context; the present work extends the empirical record with a fully specified ML pipeline, spatial analysis, and robust validation. The repository is reproducible.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} air quality; PM\textsubscript{2.5}; time series forecasting; Diebold--Mariano test; machine learning; Yerevan

\section{Introduction}

Ambient PM\textsubscript{2.5} concentrations are associated with cardiovascular and respiratory harm even below levels that produce visible smog \citep{world2021who}. In many cities, policymakers and the public benefit from timely forecasts that support health advisories, traffic measures, and communication during pollution episodes. Yerevan experiences recurring wintertime pollution linked to residential heating, topography, and meteorology; reliable short-horizon forecasts are therefore both scientifically interesting and practically relevant.

Forecasting PM\textsubscript{2.5} at hourly resolution is challenging because concentrations are persistent, seasonal, and driven by nonlinear interactions between emissions and weather. A substantial literature applies machine learning (ML) and deep learning to air quality prediction \citep{shao2020predictions}, often reporting impressive accuracy. At the same time, classical time-series models remain standard benchmarks \citep{box2015time,hyndman2021forecasting}, and comparative studies must guard against information leakage, overfitting to a single temporal split, and overinterpretation of point estimates without uncertainty.

We study four interlinked questions: (1) \emph{When} do linear autoregressive and seasonal (SARIMA) models outperform feature-rich ML? (2) How does \emph{spatial aggregation} (city, district, station) change model rankings? (3) Do tuned boosting and DeepAR alter qualitative conclusions? (4) Are differences \emph{stable over time} under walk-forward validation? We emphasize reproducibility: frozen AR coefficients, explicit split dates, block bootstrap and HAC-consistent Diebold--Mariano tests \citep{diebold1995comparing,harvey1997testing}, and code-level leakage control for new features.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item We implement a modular pipeline: controlled imputation audit, past-only lags, rolling features, and optional spatial re-aggregation to districts (metadata-based or, if needed, data-driven $k$).
  \item We compare baselines, frozen AR(24), seasonal SARIMA (fixed replication orders with tractable long-train fitting), Ridge, and gradient-boosted trees with Hyperopt, plus optional DeepAR with probabilistic metrics when intervals are available.
  \item \emph{Walk-forward} (expanding) evaluation with SARIMA in each fold and fold-wise error variance is the primary basis for ``stability'' claims, alongside a single hold-out for headline tables.
  \item We contrast city vs district \emph{vs} station-level weighted errors; permutation importance and train-set ACF/PACF help explain why linear dynamics can dominate.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Institutional context and open data}

Air quality measurements in Armenia are disseminated through the national open-data portal associated with \texttt{airquality.am}. City-average hourly series aggregate information across monitoring stations and are a pragmatic target for public communication because they summarize typical exposure conditions at urban scale. Such aggregation smooths micro-scale heterogeneity (street canyons, traffic corridors) that station-level models would emphasize; our results should therefore be read as \emph{city-representative} forecasts rather than site-specific nowcasts. From a public-health perspective, short-term forecasts can complement threshold-based alerts even when they are not causal predictors of health outcomes \citep{eea2019air}.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Forecast comparison and inference.}
\citet{diebold1995comparing} formalized tests for equal forecast accuracy; finite-sample adjustments and loss differentials are discussed by \citet{harvey1997testing}. We follow a long-run HAC variance for the mean loss differential (Bartlett--weighted autocovariances) with a lag order tied to the forecast horizon and a Newey--West-style rule, reported alongside each test.

\paragraph{Time-series and machine learning for air quality.}
Surveys highlight the diversity of ML approaches applied to PM\textsubscript{2.5} forecasting, from shallow learners to deep networks \citep{shao2020predictions}. Classical ARIMA/SARIMA models remain common benchmarks because they encode smooth persistence and seasonality \citep{box2015time}. \citet{hyndman2021forecasting} provide accessible treatment of seasonal models, cross-validation for series, and forecast evaluation.

\paragraph{Direct versus iterated multi-step forecasting.}
\citet{marcellino2006comparison} compare direct and iterated multistep autoregressive forecasts; direct methods estimate separate models for each horizon, whereas iterated methods chain one-step models. We employ direct multi-step estimation for horizons $h\in\{1,2,3,4,6,12,24\}$ hours for supervised models, which trades off model proliferation against simpler error propagation.

\paragraph{Local comparators.}
The AUA CSE monograph on air quality in Yerevan \citep{auaair2025yerevan} situates PM\textsubscript{2.5} sources, health context, and monitoring. Our contribution is methodological (forecasting protocol, feature audit, and spatial robustness) using an updated hourly panel; readers should not equate the two works' modeling targets or data vintages.

\paragraph{Interpretability.}
We complement accuracy tables with train-segment ACF/PACF plots, permutation-based importance for tuned XGBoost on a held test slice, and a district/station error comparison where sample sizes allow; impurity importances are \emph{not} primary \citep{lundberg2017unified}.

\section{Data and study area}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Source and variables}

We use open hourly air quality data for Yerevan distributed through the project data pipeline (see \texttt{DATA\_README.md} in the repository). The modeling target is city-average PM\textsubscript{2.5} ($\mu$g/m$^3$). Auxiliary variables used when sufficiently complete include PM\textsubscript{10}, temperature, pressure, and humidity. Timestamps are aligned to a regular hourly grid; gaps in the raw record motivate the imputation strategy discussed below.

\subsection{Data quality and imputation}

Real sensors exhibit downtime, calibration drift, and extreme spikes. The main pipeline uses \emph{controlled} imputation: very short runs of missing hours are filled with local temporal interpolation; longer medium-length gaps use hour-of-day medians; very long gaps are left missing where uncertainty is high, with binary flags for downstream use. A limited ffill/bfill (with a short cap) is applied only as a last resort for remaining covariate holes. This policy avoids naive forward-filling across large multi-day gaps. Missingness is audited (fractions, gap lengths, monthly patterns) and summarized in \texttt{results/json/results\_summary.json}. Complementary exploratory diagnostics (heatmaps, stationarity checks) can be generated with \texttt{scripts/data\_quality\_supplement.py} and should be summarized descriptively in revisions of this draft; that script uses a simpler fill for exploration and should not be confused with the main pipeline’s imputation module.

From a modeling standpoint, missingness in winter months may be \emph{not missing at random} if instrument outages correlate with extreme cold or maintenance schedules; we do not claim a formal MAR/MNAR identification result, but we flag the risk that imputation can bias performance toward optimistic values during problematic periods.

\subsection{Spatial structure and district-level aggregation}

The core spatial design moves beyond a single city-average series. We begin from station-level hourly PM\textsubscript{2.5}, then map stations to Yerevan districts. The implementation uses a two-stage rule: (i) if station metadata contain administrative district labels, these are used directly; (ii) if labels are missing, geographic clustering over station coordinates is used as a fallback with data-driven $k$ selection (silhouette criterion). In the final run used here, administrative district mapping was available, and clustering served as a robustness fallback rather than the primary assignment mechanism.

The station metadata include 205 uniquely geolocated Yerevan stations (0 missing coordinates). Table~\ref{tab:district_station_counts} summarizes district representation.

Coverage is clearly imbalanced: central districts (especially Kentron) are dense, while Nork-Marash and Erebuni are sparse, and Nubarashen is absent. This matters for reliability because district-level aggregates from few stations can be noisier and less representative of local micro-environments.

District aggregation is still useful for three reasons. First, it attenuates station-level sensor noise and intermittent outliers. Second, it preserves meaningful spatial heterogeneity that city-wide averaging suppresses. Third, it provides a practical middle scale between highly noisy station forecasting and heavily smoothed city forecasting. In modeling terms, city-level training uses one aggregated series, while district-level evaluation trains and evaluates model families on each district time series separately and then reports weighted summaries.

\subsection{Train, validation, and test splits}

We use a single chronological split with proportions 70\%/15\%/15\% of usable rows after listwise deletion of rows with missing targets or features. Validation is reserved for descriptive monitoring; the main results concern the \emph{test} period.

For the replication run underlying the tables in this manuscript, split boundaries and row counts match \texttt{results/json/split\_info.json} (also embedded in \texttt{results\_summary.json}). Ranges are as follows (from the last pipeline run):
\begin{itemize}
  \item Training: 2019-02-04 00:00 through 2023-12-29 01:00 (42{,}832 hourly rows in the model table).
  \item Validation: 2023-12-29 02:00 through 2025-01-14 11:00 (9{,}178 rows).
  \item Test: 2025-01-14 12:00 through 2026-01-31 22:00 (9{,}179 rows).
\end{itemize}
The test interval spans more than twelve months and includes at least one full heating season (October--April in the Northern Hemisphere), which matters for PM\textsubscript{2.5} dynamics in Yerevan.

A single hold-out is efficient for large hourly panels but does not average over multiple non-overlapping evaluation windows. The code implements an \emph{expanding} walk forward with a configurable test window and step, refitting a common zoo each fold. By default, SARIMA uses the same orders as the main split (optional per-fold \texttt{auto\_arima} search is available but slow) and linear baselines are included; gradient-boosted parameters can either be re-tuned per fold (``expensive'' option) or reuse validation-tuned values from the main 70/15/15 run (default). Per-fold DeepAR retraining is optional. Fold-wise means and variances, and a comparison table against the chronological test split, are in \texttt{results/tables/walk\_forward\_results\_1h.csv}, \texttt{walk\_forward\_aggregate\_by\_model.csv}, and \texttt{validation\_chronological\_vs\_walkforward\_1h.csv}.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Preprocessing and outliers}

We optionally cap extreme PM\textsubscript{2.5} values at a high quantile (main experiments: 99.5th percentile) to limit the influence of single spikes; sensitivity to this choice can be audited via the project's sensitivity sweep script. A rolling interquartile range rule is used to \emph{count} flagged hours for transparency, not to drop rows automatically in the main pipeline.

\subsection{Features}

The supervised models use a configurable set of \emph{strictly past} target lags, trailing rolling means/std/min/max, optional trigonometric seasonality (diurnal/annual), hour-of-day--lag interactions with met covariates when available, and short-horizon volatility (differences, trailing variance). Calendar features and a \emph{heating season} indicator (October--December, January--April) encode domain knowledge. All rolling statistics use a one-hour shift from the target hour to avoid look-ahead.

\subsection{Models}

\paragraph{Baselines.}
Persistence uses the current concentration as the one-step-ahead forecast. Seasonal naive at horizon one uses the value 24 hours earlier. A 24-hour rolling mean provides a smooth short-memory benchmark.

\paragraph{Autoregressive benchmark.}
We estimate an AR($p$) model with intercept on the \emph{training} segment only ($p=24$ in the main run) and generate one-step-ahead forecasts on later segments using fixed coefficients. This mirrors a leakage-avoidant forecasting exercise: the AR parameters are not re-estimated using test information.

\paragraph{Seasonal ARIMA class (software benchmark).}
The replication code supports a seasonal SARIMA/SARIMAX benchmark and includes it in final tables.

\subsection{SARIMA model specification}

The non-seasonal part uses $(p,d,q)$ and the seasonal part uses $(P,D,Q,s)$ with hourly seasonality $s=24$. The choice $s=24$ is physically motivated by day-night human activity and meteorological cycles that repeat at daily frequency. For replication, we fix $(2,1,2)\times(1,0,0)_{24}$, which matches information-criteria stepwise search on representative training tails for this dataset; the repository still exposes \texttt{pmdarima} search (\texttt{sarima\_order\_search}) for development. One-step forecasts are generated on validation and test periods without test leakage. Because hourly training windows can exceed twenty thousand observations, the state-space fit uses the trailing 20{,}000 hours before the forecast origin so that estimation remains numerically tractable on a laptop.

In the run summarized here, this SARIMA form implies differencing once to remove low-frequency drift ($d=1$), short-memory AR/MA corrections at lags 1--2, and one seasonal AR term at lag 24. Seasonal SARIMA then ranks between the best tree ensembles and ridge on city MAE (Table~\ref{tab:main_results}), while remaining informative as a seasonal structural benchmark relative to the frozen AR(24) specification.

\paragraph{Supervised learning.}
Ridge regression uses $\alpha=1$ with a stable solver. Default random forests, XGBoost, and LightGBM follow the repository’s baseline settings. Tree models can be tuned on the \emph{validation} window using Hyperopt (Tree-structured Parzen estimators) with mean absolute error as the inner objective; tuned parameters are reported in \texttt{results\_summary.json} and model names use a \texttt{\_tuned} suffix. For longer horizons, we also evaluate lightweight ensembles built from top validation performers (simple average and inverse-MAE weighting) to test whether model combination improves robustness when individual model rankings fluctuate by horizon. The multilayer perceptron was removed from the active pipeline in favor of a clearer benchmark set and optional DeepAR for deep learning; older tables that list an MLP should be regenerated.

\subsection{Hyperparameter optimization}

Hyperparameter optimization uses Hyperopt/TPE with MAE on the validation window as the objective. The configuration default is 80 trials per tree model; the replication freeze documented for this manuscript used \texttt{python run\_pipeline.py --skip-download --max-evals 10} (10 trials per tree family) to keep wall-clock time manageable while preserving the tuning protocol. Search spaces are broad but bounded to avoid degenerate fits: for random forests (e.g., \texttt{n\_estimators}, \texttt{max\_depth}, \texttt{min\_samples\_leaf}), for XGBoost (e.g., \texttt{n\_estimators}, \texttt{max\_depth}, \texttt{learning\_rate}, \texttt{subsample}, \texttt{colsample\_bytree}), and for LightGBM (\texttt{num\_leaves}, \texttt{min\_child\_samples}, and related tree-shape parameters).

\subsection{Multi-horizon forecasting}

For horizons $h\in\{2,3,4,6,12,24\}$, we estimate \emph{separate} regressors for each horizon (direct multi-step), retraining on the corresponding shifted target. This differs from recursively chaining one-step models; both approaches are defensible \citep{marcellino2006comparison}, and we document the choice explicitly.

\subsection{Evaluation metrics and statistical testing}

Point forecast accuracy is summarized with MAE, RMSE, and $R^2$ on the test set. Let $y_t$ denote realized PM\textsubscript{2.5} at test hour $t$ and $\hat{y}_t^{(m)}$ the forecast from model $m$. Mean absolute error is $\mathrm{MAE}_m = T^{-1}\sum_t |y_t - \hat{y}_t^{(m)}|$.

\paragraph{Diebold--Mariano testing.}
For two models $A$ and $B$, define the loss differential $d_t = |y_t - \hat{y}_t^{(A)}| - |y_t - \hat{y}_t^{(B)}|$ under absolute-error loss. Under regularity conditions, the Diebold--Mariano statistic is approximately standard normal when scaled by a consistent long-run variance estimate for $\{d_t\}$ \citep{diebold1995comparing}. We use a Newey--West style HAC variance with Bartlett weights and data-driven lag choice (horizon-aware rule), rather than a fixed lag. The null hypothesis is equal expected loss; two-sided $p$-values are reported.

\paragraph{Bootstrap MAE intervals.}
Bootstrap confidence intervals for MAE are generated with circular \emph{block} bootstrap (2{,}000 replications in the exported tables), holding fitted models fixed. Block resampling better preserves serial dependence in hourly errors than i.i.d.\ resampling. For transparency, optional i.i.d.\ companion intervals are also written, but block-bootstrap intervals are treated as primary in inference.

\subsection{Validation strategy: chronological split versus walk-forward}

The 70/15/15 chronological split remains the principal hold-out design for headline model tables and fixed-period interpretability. Walk-forward validation is the primary robustness layer: it tests whether ranking patterns persist across rolling temporal regimes, reports fold-level variance, and reduces the risk that conclusions are artifacts of one specific test window. In this paper, we therefore interpret single-split numbers together with walk-forward means and dispersions.

\subsection{Sensitivity analysis (preprocessing)}

The codebase supports sensitivity sweeps over capping rules and lag sets by reusing the same \texttt{run\_pipeline} entry point. Reporting a full grid search is beyond the scope of this draft; the main text fixes the 99.5th percentile cap and lag set $\{1,2,3,24\}$ hours. Readers verifying robustness should consult the repository's sensitivity outputs and confirm that qualitative rankings are stable under reasonable alternatives.

\subsection{Why do autoregressive models perform strongly?}

This likely occurs because the PM\textsubscript{2.5} process at short horizons is strongly persistent and approximately linear in recent history. Two signatures support this interpretation. First, ACF/PACF diagnostics show substantial short-lag dependence. Second, the 24-hour lag repeatedly appears as informative, matching daily periodicity in emissions and boundary-layer dynamics. In practical terms, when $y_{t+1}$ is largely a function of recent $y_t$ and $y_{t-24}$, AR-class models can capture a large share of predictable structure. Flexible ML can still help, but primarily when nonlinear effects are strong enough beyond lagged PM\textsubscript{2.5} and calendar terms.

\subsection{Heterogeneity across districts}

District-level performance is not uniform. Ajapnyak and Kentron are comparatively easier (AR MAE around 3.4--3.5), while Avan, Nork-Marash, and Shengavit are harder (AR MAE around 7--9+). This likely occurs because of a combination of local complexity (traffic corridors, mixed land use, industrial influence) and data representativeness differences by district station count.

ML gains are also heterogeneous but limited: in most districts, AR remains best, while tree models can approach AR in some better-sampled districts yet degrade more in sparse or highly volatile districts. The practical implication is that district forecasting quality is jointly governed by physical heterogeneity and sampling density, not only by model family.

\subsection{DeepAR comparison and uncertainty interpretation}

DeepAR (NeuralForecast backend) trains successfully in the replication environment, but its \emph{point} forecasts are far worse than classical and tree baselines on the city-average test window (Table~\ref{tab:main_results}), consistent with a mismatch between the default neural training setup and the strongly linear short-horizon dynamics of this series. When interval columns are available, probabilistic metrics in \texttt{results/json/results\_summary.json} complement MAE; readers should treat DeepAR here as an illustrative deep-learning benchmark rather than a competitive point forecaster without further architecture search.

\subsection{Error analysis}

Largest errors cluster around abrupt regime changes: sharp pollution spikes, transitions between heating-intense and cleaner periods, and locally idiosyncratic station behaviors. This likely occurs because lag-based models extrapolate recent patterns, while sudden emission/meteorology shifts violate short-memory assumptions. At city level these events are partially smoothed; at station level they dominate error tails, which explains the gap between city and station MAE.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The empirical pattern---a strong AR benchmark and competitive persistence---is consistent with high short-run autocorrelation in hourly PM\textsubscript{2.5}. When the conditional mean is close to a linear function of recent lags, penalized linear models and shallow trees may not unlock large gains relative to well-specified autoregressions, especially when flexibility is constrained by default hyperparameters chosen to limit overfitting.

Machine learning methods can approximate nonlinearities and interactions, but they also require careful tuning and can overfit when signal-to-noise is modest relative to model complexity. Under the chosen defaults, ridge and untuned XGBoost exhibit similar point MAE on the city test window (Table~\ref{tab:main_results}), while tuned boosting improves over ridge but still trails AR(24); the default export does not include an XGBoost--ridge Diebold--Mariano pair. This pattern suggests that marginal complexity above ridge is not guaranteed to beat strong linear autoregression on this split without more extensive model selection on validation data (which would need its own inferential care to avoid optimistic bias).

Domain features such as the heating-season indicator encode scientifically meaningful structure; their contribution should be interpreted alongside meteorology and emissions context. The random-forest importance plot in the replication bundle emphasizes lags and calendar variables, which aligns with intuition about diurnal activity and seasonal heating. Impurity-based importance should, however, be supplemented with permutation importance in future revisions \citep{lundberg2017unified}.

Comparisons to literature values must respect differences in stations, horizons, loss functions, and leakage controls; headline MAE numbers are not directly comparable across studies without harmonization. Relative to prior PM\textsubscript{2.5} forecasting studies that emphasize ML gains, our evidence is consistent with a recurring finding in environmental time series: when persistence and periodicity are strong, carefully specified linear baselines can remain competitive. Relative to the AUA local monograph \citep{auaair2025yerevan}, this capstone contributes a stronger forecasting protocol (walk-forward robustness, spatial stratification, and formal predictive-accuracy testing) rather than only descriptive environmental context.

Adding newer data did not overturn the central pattern in the reported run. The best one-hour model remained AR-class, and the city-to-district-to-station degradation remained monotonic. This suggests that recent additional observations increased sample breadth but did not fundamentally change the short-horizon dependence structure.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Validation design as a deliberate trade-off.} We use a chronological hold-out for interpretability and walk-forward for robustness, but not a full nested CV for every model due compute cost.
  \item \textbf{Imputation policy is explicit, not assumption-free.} Controlled short/medium/long gap handling improves practical usability but remains a modeling assumption.
  \item \textbf{Deep learning benchmark.} DeepAR runs end-to-end, but its point forecasts under the default configuration are not competitive with AR and tree models on the city-average test window; conclusions about ``best'' accuracy therefore rely on classical and tree families unless DeepAR is re-engineered for this task.
  \item \textbf{Spatial coverage imbalance.} District representation is uneven (including one unsampled district), so district-level conclusions are strongest in better-covered areas.
  \item \textbf{Predictive, not causal, objective.} The analysis is designed for forecasting performance and does not identify intervention effects.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a transparent, reproducible forecasting study that examines when and why model families differ for hourly PM\textsubscript{2.5} in Yerevan. Under strict chronology plus walk-forward robustness, AR(24) is strongest on city and district-weighted errors, seasonal SARIMA remains a useful benchmark below AR(24) on city MAE, and station-level forecasting degrades for all models due to higher local variability (with persistence winning on the weighted station sample). The results indicate that daily periodicity and short-lag persistence explain much of the predictable structure at 1-hour horizons; therefore, additional ML complexity does not automatically translate into better accuracy in this setup. The central contribution is not a leaderboard, but a mechanism-based comparison linking temporal dependence, spatial aggregation, horizon length, and model behavior.

Future work includes nested hyperparameter control under walk-forward, permutation-based feature importance, optional structural-break robustness around the COVID-19 period, and archiving a frozen dataset snapshot with a permanent DOI for full reproducibility.
